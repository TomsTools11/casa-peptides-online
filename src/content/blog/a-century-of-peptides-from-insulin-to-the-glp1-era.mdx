The story of peptide research spans more than a century, from a crude pancreatic extract that transformed diabetes research in 1922 to the GLP-1 receptor agonists generating over $140 billion in global market activity today. Peptides now represent one of the most actively studied classes of compounds in modern biochemistry, with over 80 peptide-based compounds reaching market approval and more than 600 in preclinical investigation worldwide. This article traces the key milestones in peptide research, from early isolation and sequencing through the synthesis breakthroughs that made large-scale production possible, to the modern era of targeted peptide research compounds.

## The Dawn of Peptide Science: 1902–1935

The history of peptide research begins in 1902, when physiologists William Bayliss and Ernest Starling identified secretin, the first recognized peptide hormone and the compound that gave rise to the term "hormone" itself. Their discovery established the foundational concept of chemical signaling between organs and opened an entirely new field of endocrinology research that would define much of twentieth-century biochemistry.

Two decades later came the milestone that brought peptides into global scientific focus. In 1921–1922, Frederick Banting, Charles Best, James Collip, and J.J.R. Macleod at the University of Toronto isolated insulin from bovine and porcine pancreatic tissue. Insulin became the first peptide compound applied in a clinical research context, a landmark moment that demonstrated the profound potential of peptide-based compounds and earned Banting and Macleod the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1923.

By 1935, researchers had isolated oxytocin and vasopressin from the posterior pituitary gland, revealing that peptides played regulatory roles across multiple biological systems, not just metabolic pathways. Yet a fundamental challenge remained: sourcing peptides through animal tissue extraction was resource-intensive, difficult to scale, and produced inconsistent yields. The field needed a way to build these molecules from scratch.

## The Synthesis Revolution: 1950s–1960s

The 1950s and 1960s delivered three Nobel Prize–winning breakthroughs that transformed peptide research from an extraction science into a synthetic discipline.

In 1955, Frederick Sanger completed the sequencing of insulin, revealing its two-chain, disulfide-bonded structure at the amino acid level. This was the first protein ever fully sequenced, a feat that earned Sanger the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1958 and gave researchers the architectural blueprint they needed to understand and eventually reproduce peptide structures.

A year earlier, in 1954, Vincent du Vigneaud had achieved the first complete chemical synthesis of a peptide hormone, producing oxytocin and vasopressin in the laboratory. His work, recognized with the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1955, proved a critical concept: peptides could be manufactured, not just extracted. This was a paradigm shift for the field.

The most consequential breakthrough came in 1963, when Robert Bruce Merrifield invented solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS). By anchoring amino acids to an insoluble resin bead and building the peptide chain stepwise, Merrifield reduced synthesis timelines from months to days. His method, which earned the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1984, laid the foundation for scalable, reproducible peptide production, the same core methodology that underpins research-grade synthesis today.

## Expansion and Clinical Milestones: 1970s–1990s

With synthesis capabilities in hand, the following decades saw an explosion in peptide compound identification, characterization, and research application.

The discovery of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) in 1971, a 10-amino-acid peptide, led to one of the first synthetic peptide research applications in the mid-1970s. GnRH analogs like leuprolide, which reached market approval in 1985, became widely studied compounds in hormonal research and demonstrated the commercial viability of synthetic peptide development.

A pivotal moment arrived in 1978, when Genentech produced recombinant human insulin using E. coli bacteria through recombinant DNA technology. This led to Humulin's approval in 1982 as the first biotech-derived compound, eliminating dependence on animal-sourced extraction and opening the door to producing complex peptides at scale with consistent purity.

The 1990s brought rapid identification of new peptide hormones and their receptors. Among the compounds attracting significant research interest during this period were BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound), a 15-amino-acid sequence derived from human gastric juice studied extensively in tissue repair and cytoprotective models, and GHK-Cu (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine copper complex), investigated for its roles in wound repair, collagen synthesis, and dermal remodeling research.

## The Modern Peptide Research Landscape: 2000s–Present

The twenty-first century has cemented peptides as one of the most dynamic areas of biochemical research. The 2003 approval of enfuvirtide, a 36-amino-acid peptide studied in HIV research, demonstrated that peptide compounds could address complex viral mechanisms, expanding the scope of what researchers considered possible.

Then came the GLP-1 receptor agonist era, which has reshaped the research landscape entirely. Exenatide reached market in 2005 as the first GLP-1 receptor agonist. Liraglutide followed in 2010, studied for both metabolic and weight management research. Semaglutide arrived in 2017 for metabolic research and expanded into weight management research by 2021. And in 2022, tirzepatide introduced the first dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist, a new class of multi-receptor peptide compounds representing a significant advancement in targeted peptide research.

The broader peptide research market has expanded in parallel. Over 80 peptide compounds have reached market approval globally, with more than 200 in clinical-stage investigation and over 600 in preclinical research. The global peptide market is valued at over $140 billion as of 2025, according to leading industry analyses. Beyond GLP-1 compounds, emerging classes attracting substantial research attention include growth hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, Ipamorelin), healing and recovery peptides (TB-500), anti-aging compounds (Epithalon, NAD+), and neuropeptides (Selank, Semax).

## Research Considerations for Modern Peptide Compounds

As the diversity of available research peptides has grown, so has the importance of quality verification and proper handling protocols.

Purity and quality verification remain foundational to reliable research outcomes. Third-party HPLC testing and batch-specific Certificates of Analysis (COA) provide the documentation researchers need to trust their compounds. A purity benchmark of 99% or higher, combined with GMP manufacturing standards, ensures consistent batch-to-batch quality and reproducible results.

Proper storage and handling are equally critical for maintaining peptide integrity. Temperature sensitivity, the stability differences between lyophilized and reconstituted forms, and light exposure considerations all factor into compound longevity and research reliability. Detailed documentation (COAs, batch numbers, and analytical data) serves as the backbone of reproducible research, enabling scientists to verify exactly what they are working with and trace any variability to its source.

Sourcing from verified suppliers who provide transparent testing, clear documentation, and consistent manufacturing standards is not a convenience but a prerequisite for research that produces meaningful results.
